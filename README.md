# Journ124_Final_Project
The Post-Pandemic Commute: Why Bay Area Public Transit Ridership Remains 20% Below 2022 Levels
Project Overview
This project investigates the post-pandemic recovery of public transit in the San Francisco Bay Area. While society has largely returned to normal, data from the National Transit Database reveals that ridership levels remain significantly below 2022 benchmarks. This analysis explores whether this persistent gap—roughly 20%—is an anomaly or a structural shift in how Bay Area residents approach commuting, likely driven by the adoption of hybrid work models.

Data Source
Primary Data: Federal Transit Administration (FTA) - National Transit Database (NTD)

Dataset Used: MonthlyRidershipData_SB125June2026.xlsx

Trustworthiness Statement: The NTD is the primary repository of transit data in the United States. While government-reported data is highly trustworthy, I have accounted for potential reporting delays or systemic inconsistencies by focusing strictly on the San Francisco Bay Area Rapid Transit District (BART) rail mode to ensure a "like-for-like" comparison.

Analysis Process
I performed the analysis using Google Sheets to ensure data integrity and transparency. The process involved:

Filtering: I filtered the dataset to isolate the "San Francisco Bay Area Rapid Transit District" (BART) and its primary "Heavy Rail" (HR) mode.

Data Cleaning: I restructured the wide-format dataset (months as columns) into a time-series format to facilitate aggregation. I addressed potential missing values by cross-referencing against historical trends.

Calculation: To account for seasonal variance, I calculated the year-over-year (YoY) percentage change by comparing the first six months of 2022 against the first six months of 2026.

Limitations and Ethical Considerations
Data Limitations: This dataset measures "unlinked passenger trips." It cannot distinguish between daily commuters and leisure travelers. Consequently, the observed 20% decline likely masks an even sharper drop in traditional peak-hour work commuting, partially offset by an increase in non-peak travel.

Ethical Reflection: Public transit ridership is often linked to funding levels. A persistent decline in ridership could lead to service cuts, which disproportionately impacts low-income communities and individuals who lack private vehicle access. Reporting on this topic requires sensitivity; it is essential to frame this not as a "failure" of the transit system, but as a mismatch between traditional service models and the realities of modern hybrid-work urban life.

Summary
The data confirms that the Bay Area commuting landscape has undergone a fundamental transformation. The persistent 20% ridership gap is not merely a statistical error, but a reflection of the "new normal." Future planning for Bay Area transit must move beyond the goal of "returning to 2019/2022 levels" and instead focus on creating service models that accommodate the flexible, non-linear schedules of today’s workforce.
